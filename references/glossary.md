# NIST Cybersecurity Glossary

A glossary of frequently used terms across NIST cybersecurity publications, organized alphabetically.

---

## A

**Access Control (AC)** — The process of granting or denying specific requests to obtain and use information and related information processing services; and to enter specific physical facilities. *(SP 800-53, FIPS 201)*

**Accreditation** — See *Authorization to Operate (ATO)*. The term "accreditation" was used in earlier NIST publications and has been replaced by "authorization" in current RMF terminology. *(SP 800-37)*

**Adequate Security** — Security protections commensurate with the risk resulting from the unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction of information. *(OMB Circular A-130)*

**Assessment Objective** — A set of determination statements that, taken together, express the desired outcome for the assessment of a security or privacy control. *(SP 800-53A)*

**Assessor** — See *Security Control Assessor (SCA)*.

**Attack Surface** — The set of points on the boundary of a system, a system element, or an environment where an attacker can try to enter, cause an effect on, or extract data from. *(SP 800-53)*

**Authentication** — Verifying the identity of a user, process, or device, often as a prerequisite to allowing access to resources in a system. *(SP 800-63, FIPS 200)*

**Authenticator** — Something the claimant possesses and controls (typically a cryptographic module or password) that is used to authenticate the claimant's identity. *(SP 800-63B)*

**Authenticator Assurance Level (AAL)** — A category describing the strength of the authentication process. AAL1 requires single-factor authentication, AAL2 requires multi-factor authentication, and AAL3 requires hardware-based multi-factor authentication with verifier impersonation resistance. *(SP 800-63B)*

**Authorization Boundary** — See *System Boundary*.

**Authorization to Operate (ATO)** — The official management decision given by a senior organizational official (the Authorizing Official) to authorize operation of an information system and to explicitly accept the risk to organizational operations, assets, individuals, other organizations, and the Nation based on the implementation of an agreed-upon set of security and privacy controls. *(SP 800-37)*

**Authorizing Official (AO)** — A senior federal official or executive with the authority to formally assume responsibility for operating an information system at an acceptable level of risk to organizational operations, assets, individuals, other organizations, and the Nation. *(SP 800-37)*

**Availability** — Ensuring timely and reliable access to and use of information. *(FIPS 199, 44 U.S.C., Sec. 3542)*

## B

**Baseline** — A set of security controls that has been established through information security categorization (FIPS 199) to address the security needs of a group of information systems. Three baselines exist: Low, Moderate, and High. *(SP 800-53B)*

**Body of Evidence (BoE)** — The set of documents, artifacts, and test results that provide evidence for the assessment of security controls. Typically includes the SSP, SAR, POA&M, and supporting documentation. *(SP 800-37)*

## C

**Categorization** — See *Security Categorization*.

**Cloud Service Provider (CSP)** — An organization that provides cloud computing services, including IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS. In the FedRAMP context, a CSP seeking authorization must demonstrate compliance with applicable security baselines. *(FedRAMP, SP 800-145)*

**Common Control** — A security or privacy control that is inherited by multiple information systems or programs. Common controls are implemented at the organizational level and provided to systems that inherit them. Examples include physical security controls for a facility that hosts multiple systems. *(SP 800-37, SP 800-53)*

**Compensating Control** — A management, operational, or technical control employed by an organization in lieu of a recommended security control that provides equivalent or comparable protection for an information system. Used when the prescribed control cannot be implemented. *(SP 800-53, SP 800-53B)*

**Confidentiality** — Preserving authorized restrictions on information access and disclosure, including means for protecting personal privacy and proprietary information. *(FIPS 199, 44 U.S.C., Sec. 3542)*

**Configuration Management (CM)** — A collection of activities focused on establishing and maintaining the integrity of information technology products and information systems, through control of processes for initializing, changing, and monitoring the configurations of those products and systems throughout the system development life cycle. *(SP 800-53, SP 800-128)*

**Continuous Diagnostics and Mitigation (CDM)** — A DHS/CISA program that provides federal agencies with tools and capabilities to identify cybersecurity risks on an ongoing basis, prioritize these risks based on potential impacts, and enable cybersecurity personnel to mitigate the most significant problems first. *(CISA, SP 800-207)*

**Continuous Monitoring** — Maintaining ongoing awareness of information security, vulnerabilities, and threats to support organizational risk management decisions. Includes ongoing assessment of security controls, security status monitoring, and active management of risk. *(SP 800-137, SP 800-37)*

**Control** — See *Security Control*.

**Control Enhancement** — Augmentation of a security control to build in additional (but related) functionality to the control, increase the strength of the control, or add specificity to the control. Denoted by the base control number followed by a parenthetical number, e.g., AC-2(1). *(SP 800-53)*

**Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI)** — Information the Government creates or possesses, or that an entity creates or possesses for or on behalf of the Government, that a law, regulation, or Government-wide policy requires or permits an agency to handle using safeguarding or dissemination controls. *(32 CFR Part 2002, EO 13556)*

**Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)** — A voluntary framework developed by NIST consisting of standards, guidelines, and best practices to manage cybersecurity risk. CSF 2.0 organizes outcomes into six Functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. *(NIST CSF 2.0)*

**Cybersecurity Supply Chain Risk Management (C-SCRM)** — A systematic process for managing cyber supply chain risk exposures, threats, and vulnerabilities throughout the supply chain and developing risk response strategies to the risks presented by the supplier, the supplied products and services, or the supply chain. *(SP 800-161)*

## D

**Data-at-Rest** — Data that is stored on a storage medium (e.g., hard drive, tape, SAN) and not actively being transmitted or processed. Protection typically involves encryption. *(SP 800-53, SC-28)*

**Data-in-Transit** — Data that is being transmitted over a communications network. Protection typically involves TLS, IPsec, or other encryption protocols. *(SP 800-53, SC-8)*

**Denial of Authorization to Operate (DATO)** — The official management decision to deny authorization to operate an information system. Issued when the Authorizing Official determines that the risk is unacceptable. *(SP 800-37)*

**Derived PIV Credential** — A credential issued based on proof of possession of a PIV card, intended for use on mobile and other devices where a physical PIV card reader is impractical. *(FIPS 201-3, SP 800-157)*

## E

**Enterprise Risk Management (ERM)** — An organization-wide approach to managing risk that integrates strategic planning, operations, reporting, and compliance objectives. Cybersecurity risk, including supply chain risk, is integrated into ERM. *(OMB Circular A-123, SP 800-161)*

**Examination** — An assessment method that involves reviewing, inspecting, observing, studying, or analyzing assessment objects (specifications, mechanisms, or activities). One of the three assessment methods in SP 800-53A. *(SP 800-53A)*

## F

**Federal Information Processing Standards (FIPS)** — Standards issued by NIST after approval by the Secretary of Commerce pursuant to the Federal Information Security Modernization Act (FISMA). FIPS are compulsory and binding for federal agencies. *(FISMA)*

**Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program (FedRAMP)** — A government-wide program that provides a standardized approach to security authorization for cloud products and services. FedRAMP leverages SP 800-53 baselines with cloud-specific additions. *(FedRAMP)*

**Federation** — A process that allows the conveyance of identity and authentication information across a set of networked systems. In federation, an Identity Provider (IdP) asserts identity attributes to a Relying Party (RP). *(SP 800-63C)*

**Federation Assurance Level (FAL)** — A category describing the strength of the assertion protocol used in a federated environment. FAL1 uses bearer assertions, FAL2 uses holder-of-key assertions, FAL3 adds direct presentation. *(SP 800-63C)*

**FIPS 140-3** — Federal standard specifying security requirements for cryptographic modules. Defines four security levels. Modules are validated through the CMVP. *(FIPS 140-3)*

**FIPS 199** — Federal standard for security categorization of information and information systems based on potential impact (Low, Moderate, High) to confidentiality, integrity, and availability. *(FIPS 199)*

## G

**Governance** — The system by which an organization directs and controls cybersecurity risk management. Encompasses strategy, policy, roles, responsibilities, and oversight. Introduced as the Govern (GV) function in CSF 2.0. *(CSF 2.0)*

## H

**High-Water Mark** — The method of determining the overall system security categorization by selecting the highest impact level among the three security objectives (confidentiality, integrity, availability). *(FIPS 199, SP 800-37)*

**Hybrid Control** — A security control that is implemented partly as a common control and partly as a system-specific control. The common portion is inherited; the system-specific portion is implemented by the system. *(SP 800-53)*

## I

**Identity Assurance Level (IAL)** — A category describing the strength of the identity proofing process. IAL1 requires no identity proofing, IAL2 requires remote or in-person proofing, IAL3 requires in-person or supervised remote proofing. *(SP 800-63A)*

**Identity Proofing** — The process by which a Credential Service Provider (CSP) collects, validates, and verifies information about a person to establish that the person is who they claim to be. *(SP 800-63A)*

**Impact Level** — The magnitude of harm that can be expected to result from the consequences of unauthorized disclosure, modification, or destruction of information, or loss of information system availability. Levels: Low, Moderate, High. *(FIPS 199)*

**Incident** — An occurrence that actually or imminently jeopardizes, without lawful authority, the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information or an information system; or constitutes a violation or imminent threat of violation of law, security policies, security procedures, or acceptable use policies. *(FISMA, SP 800-61)*

**Indicator of Compromise (IOC)** — A forensic artifact or observable that suggests an intrusion has occurred or is underway. Includes file hashes, IP addresses, domain names, registry keys, and behavioral patterns. *(SP 800-61, SP 800-150)*

**Information System Security Manager (ISSM)** — Individual responsible for managing the security program for an organization's information systems. Oversees ISSOs and ensures security practices are consistent across the organization. *(SP 800-37)*

**Information System Security Officer (ISSO)** — Individual responsible for ensuring the appropriate operational security posture is maintained for an information system. Serves as the principal advisor on security matters to the system owner. *(SP 800-37)*

**Integrity** — Guarding against improper information modification or destruction, and includes ensuring information non-repudiation and authenticity. *(FIPS 199, 44 U.S.C., Sec. 3542)*

**Interview** — An assessment method that involves conducting discussions with individuals or groups of individuals within an organization to facilitate assessor understanding, achieve clarification, or obtain evidence. *(SP 800-53A)*

## J

**Joint Authorization Board (JAB)** — The primary governance and decision-making body for FedRAMP. Previously comprised of CIOs from DoD, DHS, and GSA, the JAB issued Provisional ATOs (P-ATOs) for cloud services. FedRAMP has evolved its governance structure. *(FedRAMP)*

## K

**Key Management** — The activities involving the handling of cryptographic keys and other related security parameters (e.g., initialization vectors) during the entire life cycle of the keys, including their generation, storage, establishment, entry and output, use, and destruction. *(SP 800-57)*

## L

**Least Privilege** — The principle that a security architecture should be designed so that each entity is granted the minimum system resources and authorizations the entity needs to perform its function. *(SP 800-53, AC-6)*

**Likelihood** — A weighted factor based on a subjective analysis of the probability that a given threat is capable of exploiting a given vulnerability or a set of vulnerabilities. *(SP 800-30)*

## M

**Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** — Authentication using two or more different factors to achieve authentication. Factors include: something you know (password), something you have (token), something you are (biometric). *(SP 800-63B)*

## N

**Non-Repudiation** — Assurance that the sender of information is provided with proof of delivery and the recipient is provided with proof of the sender's identity, so neither can later deny having processed the information. *(SP 800-53, AU-10)*

## O

**Open Security Controls Assessment Language (OSCAL)** — A set of standardized, machine-readable formats developed by NIST for representing security control catalogs, baselines, system security plans, assessment plans, assessment results, and POA&Ms. Designed to automate security assessment and authorization processes. *(NIST OSCAL)*

**Organization-Defined Parameter (ODP)** — A variable within a security control that the implementing organization must define. For example, AC-7 requires the organization to define the number of consecutive invalid logon attempts before lockout. FedRAMP specifies many ODP values. *(SP 800-53)*

**Overlay** — A specification of security controls, control enhancements, supplemental guidance, and other supporting information employed during the tailoring process, that is intended to complement (and further refine) security control baselines. Overlays may be community-wide or technology-specific. *(SP 800-53)*

## P

**Penetration Testing** — A test methodology in which assessors, typically working under specific constraints, attempt to circumvent or defeat the security features of an information system. *(SP 800-53A, SP 800-115)*

**Personal Identity Verification (PIV)** — A system for verifying the identity of federal employees and contractors using a standard smart card. Defined by FIPS 201 and mandated by HSPD-12. *(FIPS 201)*

**Phishing-Resistant Authenticator** — An authenticator that is resistant to verifier impersonation attacks (phishing). Cryptographically binds the authentication to the relying party's identity. Examples: FIDO2/WebAuthn, PIV smart card. *(SP 800-63B, OMB M-22-09)*

**Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M)** — A document that identifies tasks needing to be accomplished to address security weaknesses or deficiencies. It details resources required to accomplish the tasks, milestones for completing the tasks, and completion dates. Also known as a corrective action plan. *(SP 800-37, OMB Circular A-130)*

**Policy Decision Point (PDP)** — In Zero Trust Architecture, the logical component responsible for making access decisions. Composed of the Policy Engine (PE) and Policy Administrator (PA). *(SP 800-207)*

**Policy Enforcement Point (PEP)** — In Zero Trust Architecture, the logical component responsible for enabling, monitoring, and terminating connections between a subject and an enterprise resource based on decisions from the PDP. *(SP 800-207)*

**Predisposing Condition** — A condition that exists within an organization, a mission or business process, enterprise architecture, information system, or environment of operation, which contributes to (i.e., increases or decreases) the likelihood that one or more threat events, once initiated, result in adverse impacts. *(SP 800-30)*

**Privileged Account** — A system account with elevated access rights or permissions beyond those of a standard user. Includes administrator, root, and service accounts with broad system access. *(SP 800-53)*

**Provisional Authorization to Operate (P-ATO)** — An initial approval of a cloud service provider's security package by the FedRAMP Joint Authorization Board (JAB). A P-ATO is not a full authorization; individual agencies leverage the P-ATO and issue their own ATOs. *(FedRAMP)*

## R

**Residual Risk** — The risk that remains after security controls have been implemented. The Authorizing Official accepts residual risk when issuing an ATO. *(SP 800-37, SP 800-30)*

**Risk** — A measure of the extent to which an entity is threatened by a potential circumstance or event, and typically a function of: (i) the adverse impacts that would arise if the circumstance or event occurs; and (ii) the likelihood of occurrence. *(SP 800-30)*

**Risk Acceptance** — A risk response strategy in which the Authorizing Official or other designated official accepts the residual risk associated with operating an information system. Documented in the authorization decision. *(SP 800-37, SP 800-39)*

**Risk Assessment** — The process of identifying risks to organizational operations, assets, individuals, other organizations, and the Nation, resulting from the operation of an information system. Includes threat identification, vulnerability identification, likelihood determination, impact determination, and risk determination. *(SP 800-30)*

**Risk Executive (Function)** — An individual or group within an organization that helps to ensure that risk-related considerations for individual information systems are viewed from an organization-wide perspective. *(SP 800-37)*

**Risk Management Framework (RMF)** — A structured approach to managing risk consisting of seven steps: Prepare, Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, and Monitor. Mandatory for federal information systems under FISMA. *(SP 800-37)*

**Risk Response** — The actions an organization takes to address identified risks. Four primary strategies: accept, avoid, mitigate, share/transfer. *(SP 800-39)*

## S

**Sanitization** — A process to render access to target data on media infeasible for a given level of effort. Methods include clearing (logical techniques), purging (overwriting), and destroying (physical destruction). *(SP 800-88)*

**Security Assessment Plan (SAP)** — A document that describes the objectives, scope, and methodology for assessing the security controls in an information system. The SAP is prepared before the assessment begins. *(SP 800-37, SP 800-53A)*

**Security Assessment Report (SAR)** — A document that provides the results of assessing the security controls in an information system. The SAR identifies control deficiencies, their severity, and risk to the organization. *(SP 800-37, SP 800-53A)*

**Security Categorization** — The process of determining the security category for information or an information system by assigning potential impact values (Low, Moderate, High) to the security objectives of confidentiality, integrity, and availability. *(FIPS 199)*

**Security Control** — The safeguards or countermeasures prescribed for an information system or an organization to protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the system and its information. *(SP 800-53, FIPS 200)*

**Security Control Assessor (SCA)** — An individual, group, or organization responsible for conducting a comprehensive assessment of the management, operational, and technical security controls and control enhancements employed within or inherited by an information system. *(SP 800-37)*

**Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)** — A formal, machine-readable inventory of software components and dependencies, information about those components, and their hierarchical relationships. Used to track supply chain risks and vulnerabilities. *(EO 14028, SP 800-161)*

**System Boundary** — The scope of the information system that is subject to the authorization decision. Also called the authorization boundary. Defines what is included in the ATO and what is inherited from other authorized systems. *(SP 800-37)*

**System of Records** — A group of any records under the control of any agency from which information is retrieved by the name of the individual or by some identifying number, symbol, or other identifier assigned to the individual. Governed by the Privacy Act of 1974. *(Privacy Act, SP 800-53 PT family)*

**System Security Plan (SSP)** — A formal document that provides an overview of the security requirements for an information system and describes the security controls in place or planned for meeting those requirements. The SSP describes the system, its boundary, the operational environment, how controls are implemented, and the roles and responsibilities of security personnel. *(SP 800-37, SP 800-18)*

## T

**Tailoring** — The process of customizing a security control baseline to meet the specific needs of an organization or information system. Includes scoping, selecting compensating controls, assigning parameter values, and supplementing with additional controls. *(SP 800-53B, SP 800-37)*

**Test** — An assessment method that involves exercising one or more assessment objects under specified conditions to compare actual with expected behavior. The most direct form of evidence for control effectiveness. *(SP 800-53A)*

**Third-Party Assessment Organization (3PAO)** — An independent organization accredited to conduct security assessments on behalf of cloud service providers seeking FedRAMP authorization. 3PAOs are accredited by the American Association for Laboratory Accreditation (A2LA). *(FedRAMP)*

**Threat** — Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact organizational operations, assets, individuals, other organizations, or the Nation through an information system via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification of information, or denial of service. *(SP 800-30)*

**Threat Source** — The intent and method targeted at the intentional exploitation of a vulnerability or a situation and method that may accidentally trigger a vulnerability. Categories: adversarial, accidental, structural, environmental. *(SP 800-30)*

## V

**Vulnerability** — A weakness in an information system, system security procedures, internal controls, or implementation that could be exploited or triggered by a threat source. *(SP 800-30, SP 800-53)*

**Vulnerability Scanning** — A technique used to identify hosts/host attributes and associated vulnerabilities. Typically automated using tools such as Nessus, Qualys, or Tenable. *(SP 800-53 RA-5, SP 800-115)*

## Z

**Zero Trust** — A security model that eliminates implicit trust in any one element, node, or service and instead requires continuous verification of the operational picture via real-time information from multiple sources to determine access and other system responses. *(SP 800-207)*

**Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA)** — An enterprise cybersecurity architecture based on zero trust principles. Designed to prevent unauthorized access, contain lateral movement, and reduce breach impact. Key components include the Policy Decision Point (PDP) and Policy Enforcement Point (PEP). *(SP 800-207)*
